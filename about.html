Understanding Different Types of Sensory Needs and Promoting Inclusion.

Every child experiences the world differently. Sensory needs—like sensitivity to light, sound, touch, or internal signals such as balance or hunger—can affect how children learn, communicate, and participate. Some may feel overwhelmed, while others seek sensory input to feel regulated.

Creating inclusive spaces means being flexible: offering quiet areas, adjustable lighting, fidgets or visuals, and predictable routines. By respecting individual preferences and making thoughtful accommodations, we help children feel confident, supported, and genuinely welcomed.

Autism Spectrum Condition

Sensitivity to noise, light, textures, or movement. Signs may include covering ears, avoiding certain fabrics or foods, or seeking repetitive movement.

ADHD

May involve high movement needs and difficulty filtering background noise. Signs include fidgeting, seeking tactile input, and struggling to focus in busy spaces.

Sensory Processing Difficulties

Over- or under-responsiveness to sensory input. Signs can include strong reactions to touch or sound, craving deep pressure, or difficulty with temperature and texture changes.

Developmental Delay

May involve difficulty regulating sensory input or adapting to new environments. Signs include overwhelm in busy settings and coordination challenges.

Down Syndrome

Sensory needs may relate to proprioception and muscle tone. Signs include seeking deep pressure, sensitivity to loud spaces, and benefit from visual or tactile cues.

Cerebral Palsy

Variations in muscle tone can affect touch, balance, and spatial awareness. Signs may include sensitivity to movement, preference for predictable environments, or coordination difficulties.

Learning Disabilities

Visual or auditory overload may occur. Signs include difficulty in busy classrooms and trouble following spoken instructions in noisy environments.

Trauma or PTSD

May involve heightened sensitivity to certain sensory triggers. Signs include avoidance of crowded spaces and strong startle responses.

Anxiety-Related Conditions

Sensory sensitivity may increase during anxiety. Signs include discomfort in loud or bright spaces and relief from calming sensory tools.
